Santa Fe, NM

Santa Fe, NM – the second oldest city in the United States and at 7,000 feet above sea level, the highest capital city in the country.

Santa Fe isn’t a big city but it has an Old World feeling when you stroll in downtown.

First, had lunch at a Chinese restaurant where the food tasted very good and plate presentation was decent.

Then we went to the historic sites: The oldest house and oldest church in the US. The Palace of the Governors, the oldest government building in the USA. Under the building’s portal (the overhang) you can buy Native American handcrafts as a souvenir of your visit, directly from the artisans who crafted them.

Santa Fe, New Mexico’s capital, sits in the Sangre de Cristo foothills. It’s renowned for its Pueblo-style architecture and as a creative arts hotbed. Founded as a Spanish colony in 1610, it has at its heart the traditional Plaza. The surrounding historic district’s crooked streets wind past adobe landmarks.

The “City of Faith” is also home to a few miracles, like the Loretto Chapel’s circular wooden steps. Built by an unidentified man who is said to have shown up at the chapel in 1879 with a donkey and a toolbox, his staircase has two 360-degree turns, no visible means of support and wooden pegs instead of metal nails. Some faithful at the time believed that the mystery man was St. Joseph.

The unofficial state question: “red or green,” is asking which type of chile you want your food smothered in. If you answer “Christmas,” you’ll get both. Great and reasonably priced places to sample New Mexican food.

In this picture, this senior couple touched me. They walked on the street and the husband held the wife hand. They show their love and care to each other. I can see their happiness and peace. The hand-made rugs in this store look beautiful but this couple is extremely beautiful and in love. Nothing is more important than love.

Kasha-Katuwe Tent Rocks National Monument

The texture of pure gypsum sand is like no other sand. It is fine powder-soft texture to shift through your fingers.

An unique nature wonder

you see legions of cone-shaped rock formations among beige, pink and white-colored cliffs.

The name Kasha-Katuwe, meaning “white cliffs,” comes from the traditional Keresan language of the Cochiti Pueblo inhabitants who live nearby

Hiking at Tent Rocks

Hiked 4.5 miles at Kasha-Katuwe Tent Rocks National Monument, New Mexico.

Canyon Trail

If you’re up for a challenge and some rewarding views, this is the path for you.The 3-mile round trip (out and back) on the Canyon Trail first takes you along a sandy path through a mixture of evergreens and desert landscape. The finely balanced boulders towering high above the trail are an intimidating but awe-inspiring sight. About a half-mile into your journey, you’ll begin to experience the amazing contrast of light and shadow that is unique to slot canyons. Wandering through this narrow, contoured arroyo is a spectacular treat. Along the rock-strewn corridor, you’ll have the chance to marvel at the exposed root system of a mighty ponderosa pine.

Once you emerge from the slender gorge, prepare for a climb that would make the Tin Man’s heart beat out of his chest…if he only had one. The 630 feet of elevation gain to the top of the mesa may cause you to click your heels three times and long for home but hang in there.

Once you reach the apex of the path, you will be treated to a visual feast that includes the Tent Rocks below as well as the Rio Grande Valley and the Sangre de Cristo, Jemez and Sandia Mountains. Once you’ve caught your breath and snapped all the photos you care to take, you can descend the trail and enjoy the journey in reverse on your way back to the parking lot.

Cave Loop Trail

If the steep ascent and dizzying heights of the Canyon Trail cause your courage to waver like the Cowardly Lion, fear not. The Cave Loop Trail (1.2 miles long) will still provide you with an excellent opportunity to explore Tent Rocks. From the parking lot, you follow the same trail toward the slot canyon for the first half-mile. Then at the junction, turn left, and you’ll be on your way along the fairly level ground to the cave for which this trail is named.

Before you arrive at this ancient dwelling, you should notice both the cholla and prickly pear variety of cactus. Cholla is a tall, “stick-man”-looking cactus with neon pink blooms followed by yellow fruit. Prickly pear is a smaller, ground-level cactus with lots of pads and purple fruit.​

Once at the cave, you might wonder why it is so high off the ground. Apparently ancestral Native Americans preferred caves that were above ground level because they stayed dry during storms, were more difficult for animals to enter and provided a view of the surrounding territory in case of enemy attack. The small size of the cave opening is because ancestral Native American adults were shorter than they are today. If you climb up to the opening you’ll see smoke stains on the ceiling, a sure-fire indicator that the cave was indeed used by these ancestral peoples. After your cave visit, complete the loop by descending the trail back down to the parking lot.

The Kasha-Katuwe Tent Rocks National Monument is a remarkable outdoor laboratory, offering an opportunity to observe, study, and experience the geologic processes that shape natural landscapes. The National Monument, on the Pajarito Plateau in north-central New Mexico, includes a national recreation trail and ranges from 5,570 feet to 6,760 feet above sea level. It is for foot travel only, and contains two segments that provide opportunities for hiking, birdwatching, geologic observation, and plant identification.

The cone-shaped tent rock formations are the products of volcanic eruptions that occurred 6 to 7 million years ago and left pumice, ash, and tuff deposits over 1,000 feet thick. Tremendous explosions from the Jemez volcanic field spewed pyroclasts (rock fragments), while searing hot gases blasted down slopes in an incandescent avalanche called a pyroclastic flow.

5/26/2018 – Journey to White Climbing, smiling, laughing and making it an adventure to remember! White Sands National Monument in New Mexico has to be one of the most beautiful places in the United States. I had been to other sand dunes, Death Valley Sand Dunes in CA, Sleeping Bear and Silver Lake Sand Dunes in MI, and Indiana Dunes in IN. However, this is really the most beautiful Dunes that I have ever seen in my life. The weather was hot and windy but the sky was blue with little clouds in the afternoon. It would be a perfect timing to explore here at this moment! The further we drove into the dunes, the less we could see of the road, and at some point it was covered in sand completely. We were in a white world! It was somewhat surreal, since had been driving through the desert of New Mexico for a while without even seeing any sand dunes yet, and within a couple of minutes, I suddenly was surrounded by them completely. What a such incredible wonderland! No words can express what I saw at the moment but immerse into this atmosphere. After climbed to the top of a bright white sand dune, the only things I saw were blue sky and white sand. This was incredibly beautiful. I felt the freedom in this white world. I could forget everything and just walked on this soft white sand forever with my barefoot. I started running to next dune and looked the endless white sands. What a wonderful moment that I had now. Travel opened my eyes to see the world and scenery opened my heart to feel the special moment! The dunes sit in the Tularosa Basin and had actually been part of a massive lake during the last ice age. After the lake dried out, it left a large area of gypsum on the surface, which were broken into sand-size grains over thousands of years through erosion and weathering. The winds that blow over the basin eventually formed the sand dunes. Depending on wind and weather, they actually change their shapes all the time. This is why it is nearly impossible for any plants to grow in White Sands – if the plants are not very resistant and fast-growing, they get blown over by the moving sand quickly and don’t survive.

I had planned to hike the Alkali Flat Trail (4.5 mile round-trip, at least 3 hours). By the time I reached the trail head, it was close to 90F and the desert sand blew on my face that made me barely could open my eyes. The further I walked into the dunes, the quieter it got without seeing the trail markers. This made me nervous to continue my hiking because the wind would erase my footprint and I would get lost in the middle of white desert easily. So I decided to just play at nearby the parking lot and always looked around to remember the way I had walked through. In fact, several hikers have gone lost in White Sands, overestimating their ability to walk without water or underestimating the heat.

Followed the 8-mile scenic drive and stopped at the ‘heart of the sand’ area that was a filmed area, like the video of Puff Daddy’s ‘best friend’. The peace and quiet were very relaxing.

Did some photo shooting as well. Since it was a beautiful and unique landscape, even though I don’t have any photographer’s professional experience, I was still able to take some postcard like pictures with my Nikon D5600.

After stayed in the park for about 3 hours that the heat worn me out. Drove to the hotel without watching the sunset that didn’t make me to feel regret at all. I saw and did everything I wanted in White Sands National Monument. I got an unforgettable afternoon during this Memorial Holiday in year 2018!

Pistachio Tour in NM

It is easy to get a roadside attraction when you have a cross-country trip. Saw a billboard about Pistachio Land. So decided to visit this wonderful and unplanned place as a side trip!

Now I know Pistachios are not nuts. The greenish meat inside split shell is seed of a fruit. Just like peaches or plums where an outer freshly part surrounds a shell with seed inside. In the case of pistachios, we eat the seed inside the pit.

Pistachios are now commercially grown in California, Arizona, and New Mexico. The tour guide was friendly and has sense of humor. He told us that male pistachio trees are not nuts and female pistachio trees are nuts. That means male tree doesn’t have fruits and only female trees produce fruits! Usually male trees are taller than female trees.

The flowers grow that require both male and female trees to ensure production. The fruits get ripening in the autumn.

After the tour, shopped the store. Sampled many kinds of flavor pistachios and had a pistachio ice cream to comple this tour!

A botanical journey of touring the pistachio orchard in NM. Learning the fantastic history of ancient tree and sampled flavored pistachios.
